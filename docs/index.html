LiteBoard

An inexpensive, repairable, self-contained DMX512 lighting console — feature-complete for small-to-medium theatre companies, without the four- and five-figure price tags of industry-grade boards.

Department of Computer Science & Engineering · The University of Texas at Arlington

Problem & Mission

Why we're building this.

The problem

Digital lighting consoles are essential for modern theatre, yet commercial boards from manufacturers like ETC routinely cost thousands to tens of thousands of dollars. Smaller theatres end up renting venues just to access a lightboard — and each vendor uses a proprietary, hard-to-learn showfile format.

Our approach

Design, build, and test a DMX512-compatible console that is cheap, user-friendly, and feature-complete for small-to-medium productions. Ship it three ways: fully assembled, as a parts kit, or as open schematics plus software — all usable with free and open-source tooling.

Who it's for

Educational theatre, collegiate theatre, community theatre, smaller regional theatre, Off-Off-Broadway, and touring companies that want to bring their own familiar console to any DMX512-equipped venue.

Value proposition

A senior-undergraduate thesis that demonstrates our technical and communication skills to the department, employers, and ourselves — and gives UTA CSE a showcase project that could also serve the Theatre Arts & Dance Department as a backup board or training aid.

System Overview

The console is a desktop-style machine with physical controls on the top face and I/O on the back. Inside, a single-board computer coordinates a DMX controller, a controls board, and an expansion header.

Single-Board Computer

Runs the showfile editor/executor, hosts the GUI for an external monitor, and talks to the controls board, DMX controller, and expansion header over GPIO.

DMX512 Controller

Dedicated hardware that takes instructions from the SBC and emits standards-compliant DMX512 signals at correct format and signal strength. Has its own driver, developed separately.

Controls Board

Houses all analog/digital sliders, rotary knobs, keypad buttons, and a backup LCD. Designed to lift out as one piece for easy repair after the caps are removed.

Expansion Header Board

Attach additional hardware — extra slider banks, a joystick for moving-light control, or future modules — without redesigning the core console.

Showfile Editor (GUI)

Create/save showfiles, edit cues, map device parameters to sliders and knobs, reorder cues, and launch playback. Also runs on regular PCs so technicians can prep shows away from the console.

Showfile Executor

Runs compiled showfiles on the console. Cue transitions are per-device — one light can slow-fade while another snaps instantly — with manual overrides from the physical controls.

Built-in Physical Controls

What lives on the top face of the unit.

9+ vertical sliders

Manual control of device parameters, one dedicated as a grandmaster (globally scales intensity by default).

4+ rotary knobs

Mapped per-cue to device parameters for precise live adjustment.

40+ keypad switches

Expedited access to the most common editing and playback functions without keyboard/mouse.

LCD display

Current cue, next cue, slider/knob mappings, and error codes — usable without an external monitor.

GO! button

Single-press cue advance — the defining interaction of a modern lightboard.

Guarded power & reset

Recessed/guarded switches for emergency restart without accidental triggers mid-show.

Customer Requirements

The five pillars driving every design decision.

Priority: High

Affordability

Targets: < $1000 assembled, < $500 as a kit, < $100 as schematics + software. Parts must be commonly stocked to avoid bulk-order or vendor-lock pricing.

Priority: High

Ease of Use

Intuitive interface, clear labeling, responsive feedback. Must accommodate operators of varying technical experience. Reference: ISO 9241-210 (Human-Centered Design for Interactive Systems).

Priority: Critical

Interoperability

Full DMX512 compliance via industry-standard XLR-5 connectors. Standards: ANSI E1.11 (USITT DMX512-A), ANSI E1.20 (RDM), USITT pin-out guidelines.

Priority: High

Portability

Compact, < 15 lb including casing. Must fit standard equipment cases and survive theatrical transit. Reference: ISO 11228-1 (Ergonomics, Manual Handling).

Priority: Future

Expandability

Modular hardware and software so future fader banks, wireless DMX, or plugin modules can be added without redesign. Reference: ANSI E1.11, IEEE 1471.

Priority: Critical

Performance & Safety

Controls must feel instantaneous; cue switches reflect in target devices immediately. Overcurrent protections, ESD tolerance, and IEC 62368-1 as an electrical-safety reference.

Three Ways to Get a LiteBoard

All three packaging options use free and open-source software — no DRM, no licensing fees.

Critical priority

Fully Assembled

Ready-to-use LiteBoard with power supply, recovery flash drive, manuals flash drive, and a quick-setup pamphlet. Target price < $1000.

Medium priority

Parts Kit

All parts and hardware needed to assemble a LiteBoard, with manuals, recovery image, and printed parts list. Assembly needs only basic hand tools and soldering. Target < $500.

Low priority

Schematics + Software

For users who can't ship hardware to their region — full schematics, recovery image, manuals, and parts list for self-sourcing. Target < $100.

Top Risks & Mitigations

From the project charter risk register. Likelihood/impact rated L/M/H, with severe (S) reserved for rare catastrophic impact.

R1 — Key ordered parts arrive late (M / H)

Mitigation: order materials early. Contingency: source local substitutes.

R3 — Unexpected thermal design issues (L / H)

Mitigation: prefer lower-power devices. Contingency: heatsinks and air cooling.

R4 — Unexpected signal design issues (M / M)

Mitigation: simulate DMX cable and hardware signal strength early. Contingency: oscilloscope and multimeter analysis.

R6 — Lack of time from outside commitments (H / M)

Mitigation: complete tasks well before due dates. Contingency: honest, early communication of emergent commitments.

R9 — Approval to borrow DMX devices from Theatre Arts not granted (L / S)

Mitigation: communicate good stewardship. Contingency: fabricate example test lights and DMX hardware in-house.

R10 — Prototype demonstration with TAAD not granted (H / L)

Mitigation: communicate value of the project. Contingency: internal demonstration or find a willing theatre company elsewhere.
